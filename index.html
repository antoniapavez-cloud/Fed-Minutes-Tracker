Executive Summary
  • Rate held at 3.50–3.75% on a unanimous 12–0 vote; the postmeeting statement was shortened and no longer includes the easing-bias language.
  • Leadership change: Kevin Warsh takes over as Chairman; Jerome Powell remains a voting member.
  • Inflation still rising: May PCE estimated at 4.1% (core 3.4%), up from April's 3.8% (core 3.3%), on tariffs, Middle East-linked energy costs, and the AI buildout.
  • Communications overhaul: the Chairman outlined five task forces to review the Committee's conduct of monetary policy.
01
Market Developments and Open Market Operations
Mideast optimism eases oil and inflation gauges; equities and yields still climb on AI.
  • Oil futures and near-term inflation compensation fell materially on optimism around a Middle East resolution and a US–Iran memorandum of understanding; expected policy rates, Treasury yields, the dollar, and equities all rose.
  • Policy rate expectations: markets and the Desk survey see no change in June; the median path implies no change through early 2027 and one cut in Q2 2027. Market pricing implies one hike by mid-2027, likely boosted by term premiums.
  • Treasury yields: the 10-year rose ~20bp since April and ~50bp since the conflict began; ownership has shifted somewhat toward more price-sensitive private investors.
  • Equities: the S&P 500 rose nearly 6% intermeeting, led by tech on higher earnings expectations; IPO activity is picking up to help fund AI infrastructure.
  • Private credit: BDC inflows slowed notably in Q2; net inflows likely turning more negative amid rising redemptions.
  • International: the 2-year Treasury yield rose more than similar AFE yields; the dollar appreciated modestly on widening rate differentials.
  • Money markets: generally stable, though repo rates dropped 15bp below IORB in mid-May (EFFR −2bp, the first such move since November); modest ON RRP take-up confirmed the floor held. Reserves remain in the ample range.
The Committee unanimously ratified the Desk's domestic transactions. There were no foreign currency interventions.

  • Core goods inflation up on tariffs and AI-related price pressures; core services roughly stable (housing deceleration offset by firmer nonhousing services).

03
Staff Review of the Financial Situation
Stronger data lifts yields and equities; AI optimism offsets the drag from higher rates.
  • The policy rate path and Treasury yields moved higher on stronger-than-expected data; policy-path uncertainty rose, partly on a higher term premium. Short-term inflation compensation fell but stayed slightly elevated; longer-term measures remained anchored.
  • Equities rose notably on robust earnings and AI optimism despite higher yields; the VIX slipped below its historical median and corporate spreads narrowed.
  • Foreign markets moved on prospects – then confirmation – of a Strait of Hormuz reopening agreement; AFE policy expectations and inflation compensation fell, though markets still priced further hikes in the euro area and UK. The dollar rose on widening rate differentials.
  • Credit remained accommodative for large firms and municipalities, somewhat restrictive for small businesses and households. Leveraged loan issuance picked up to a robust pace, while the private credit market kept slowing.
04
Staff Economic Outlook
Inflation forecast revised up again; growth seen a touch lower than in April.
  • Inflation forecast for this year and next revised up from April on incoming data, higher energy/input costs from the conflict, and AI-buildout effects on consumer prices. Total inflation seen slowing in H2 as gasoline prices ease, though core stays little changed this year; inflation steps down next year as tariff effects wane, reaching ~2% in 2028.
  • GDP outlook a bit lower than April's, mostly on incoming data: growth near potential this year and slightly above potential over the next two years, on strong productivity, AI capex and supportive financial conditions. Unemployment stays near its longer-run rate through next year, edging down slightly in 2028.
  • Uncertainty remains elevated (Middle East conflict, AI investment/adoption effects). Risks to employment/GDP tilted somewhat to the downside; inflation risks more skewed to the upside – persistence remains the staff's most salient risk.
05
Participants' Views
All support holding the rate; the statement is shorter and drops the easing-bias language.
Inflation ↑ (Rising)
  • Inflation increased further, well above 2%, on lingering tariff effects, Strait of Hormuz-related supply disruptions, and strong AI-driven demand. Price pressures broadened (transportation, airfares, petrochemicals, agricultural inputs); services inflation excluding housing stayed high. Medium/long-term expectations still seen near 2%.
Labor Market → (Stable)
  • Unemployment stayed near its longer-run level; payroll gains roughly matched labor force growth. Other indicators (openings, claims, layoffs) pointed to a balanced but less dynamic market. Most saw the labor market as not currently a source of inflation pressure.
Activity ↑ (Solid)
  • Solid expansion despite elevated uncertainty, led by AI-related business investment and resilient consumption (helped by stock gains and tax refunds for higher-income households); lower-income households leaned more on credit amid high gas and grocery prices.
Monetary Policy → (Hold)
  • All participants supported holding the rate. Upside inflation risks stayed elevated while downside employment risks eased slightly; a few saw a case for hiking but backed holding for now. Most preferred not to repeat the easing-bias language and favored a shorter statement; the Chairman outlined five task forces to review the Committee's broader conduct of monetary policy.

Executive Summary
  • Rate held at 3.50–3.75%, with the Committee "attentive to the risks to both sides" of its dual mandate.
  • Inflation rising: March PCE estimated at 3.5% (core 3.2%), driven by energy prices tied to the Middle East conflict.
  • Growing internal split: one dissent for a 25bp cut (Miran) and three votes against the easing-bias language (Hammack, Kashkari, Logan).
  • Financial stability: staff still rates system vulnerabilities as "notable" – elevated hedge fund leverage and private credit strain.
01
Market Developments and Open Market Operations
The Mideast conflict still dominates asset prices; rates and the dollar ease after the ceasefire.
  • Equities more than reversed their earlier declines; 2- and 10-year Treasury yields rose further; the dollar retraced part of its earlier appreciation.
  • Oil: futures curve higher than in March, though its forecasting record is mixed.
  • Inflation expectations: near-term expectations rose again; longer-term measures remain anchored near 2%.
  • Policy rate expectations: markets see little change this year; options imply ~30% odds of a hike by Q1 2027. The Desk survey points to two 25bp cuts, but later than before (Q3/Q4 2026 and Q1 2027).
  • Private credit: signs of stabilization despite continued redemptions.
  • Money markets: conditions stable; reserves remain in the ample range.
  • Swap lines: unanimous renewal with Canada, the UK, Japan, the ECB and Switzerland, plus the North American Framework with Mexico and Canada.
The Committee unanimously ratified the Desk's domestic transactions. There were no foreign currency interventions.

03
Staff Review of the Financial Situation
Markets resilient despite the conflict; financial vulnerabilities remain "notable."
  • The implied policy rate path rose modestly on conflict-related inflation concerns.
  • Equities rose despite geopolitical volatility, supported by solid earnings; corporate spreads tightened.
  • Foreign markets swung sharply; sentiment improved after the ceasefire, weighing on the dollar.
  • Credit conditions restrictive for small businesses and households; accommodative for large firms and municipalities.
  • April SLOOS: further easing in lending standards, especially for consumer loans.
Financial Stability Assessment
  • System vulnerabilities remain rated "notable": housing valuations near historic highs, elevated leverage at hedge funds (Treasury trades) and life insurers. Bank capital ratios remain high historically, though market-adjusted ratios have declined. Funding risk is moderate (low reliance on uninsured deposits).
04
Staff Economic Outlook
Growth a bit stronger than in March; inflation rises this year, returns to ~2% by late 2027.
  • Real GDP projected slightly above potential, supported by favorable financial conditions and AI-related capital spending. Unemployment near its longer-run rate through 2027, edging down slightly in 2028.
  • This year's inflation forecast revised up on the conflict and energy prices, with disinflation expected after H1.
  • Uncertainty remains elevated (conflict plus AI adoption). Downside risks to employment/GDP; upside risks to inflation – persistence is the staff's most salient risk.
